Lesson 1

Introduction to Color Science

Learn the fundamental concepts of color, light, and human vision that form the foundation of modern color science.

What is Color?

Color is one of the most familiar experiences in everyday life. We use color to recognize objects, interpret information, communicate ideas, and appreciate art and nature. Although colors appear to be properties of objects, they are actually visual sensations created by the interaction between light, an object, and the human visual system.

Without light there is no color. When light strikes an object, some wavelengths are absorbed while others are reflected or transmitted. The reflected light enters the eye, stimulates the retina, and is interpreted by the brain as different colors.

The Three Components of Color

Every color that we perceive depends on three essential elements.

  • Light Source
    Provides the spectral energy that illuminates an object.
  • Object
    Absorbs, reflects, or transmits different wavelengths of light.
  • Observer
    The human eye and brain interpret the reflected light as color.

Why Study Color Science?

Color science combines physics, optics, engineering, mathematics, psychology, and physiology to understand and accurately reproduce color. It is essential in many modern technologies.

  • Display technology
  • Television and cinema
  • Digital cameras
  • Computer vision
  • Medical imaging
  • Printing
  • Lighting engineering
  • Remote sensing
  • Optical communications

Course Roadmap

In this course you will learn how humans perceive color, how scientists developed mathematical models to describe color, and how modern imaging and display systems reproduce colors accurately.

The course begins with human vision, continues through the Wright–Guild experiments and the CIE 1931 Standard Observer, and concludes with color spaces, chromaticity diagrams, color difference, and display technologies.
